Indian Army History

The Indian Army is a beacon of courage, resilience, and selfless service. From ancient warfare to modern defense strategies, it has been pivotal in protecting the sovereignty of India. With over 1.4 million active personnel, it is the world’s second-largest standing army and an integral part of the nation’s defense framework.

World Wars

During both World War I and World War II, the Indian Army played a crucial and heroic role, contributing over 2.5 million soldiers to the Allied forces. Indian soldiers fought in diverse theaters, from the Western Front to North Africa, Italy, and Southeast Asia. Their courage and sacrifice were instrumental in the Allied victory, despite being under colonial rule at the time. Indian soldiers earned numerous decorations for bravery and valor, with heroes like Subedar Major Puran Singh and Major Dhyan Chand emerging as symbols of their indomitable spirit.

Indian Independence

The Indian Army played a critical role in India’s struggle for independence, not only in the fight against colonial forces but also in preserving internal stability. As part of the Quit India Movement and other uprisings, soldiers and patriots took up arms against British forces. In addition, India's military strength was vital during the post-independence period, ensuring the smooth transfer of power and securing borders amidst external threats. India's independence was secured thanks to the valor of its military, even as the Indian Army was also key in upholding the unity and integrity of the newly formed nation.

First Kashmir War (1947)

In 1947, when Pakistan sent tribal forces to invade Jammu and Kashmir, India’s heroic intervention under the leadership of the Indian Army saved the region from invasion. Indian forces, though initially unprepared and outnumbered, fought bravely to defend Kashmir, successfully holding key territories despite adverse conditions. The Indian Army’s resolve in securing Kashmir, particularly during the Battle of Shalateng and the push to the Jhelum River, was critical in maintaining the region's sovereignty, ultimately leading to the ceasefire brokered by the United Nations.

Annexation of Hyderabad (1948)

In 1948, after the princely state of Hyderabad refused to join India post-independence, the Indian Army launched Operation Polo to annex it. The operation, which lasted for five days, saw Indian troops display remarkable discipline, efficiency, and bravery in overcoming armed resistance from the Nizam's forces. The swift and decisive action of the Indian Army ensured that Hyderabad acceded to India, securing the region’s integration into the newly formed nation without prolonged conflict.

Assistance during the Korean War (1950–1953)

India’s heroic role in the Korean War wasn’t defined by direct combat but by its diplomatic and humanitarian contributions. India sent the 60th Indian Infantry Brigade as part of the United Nations forces, providing essential support to maintain peace and stability. Indian soldiers, led by General K.S. Thimayya, were praised for their professionalism and courage, and India’s consistent efforts to negotiate peace also demonstrated the nation’s commitment to global stability. India’s role in the Korean War helped cement its reputation as a peacekeeping nation.

Annexation of Goa, Daman and Diu (1961)

In 1961, when Portugal refused to relinquish its colonial possessions in Goa, Daman, and Diu, India’s heroic military intervention led to the peaceful annexation of these territories. The Indian Army launched Operation Vijay, swiftly defeating Portuguese forces in a matter of days. Indian forces showed immense discipline and bravery while encountering little resistance, and their swift victory not only liberated Goa but also put an end to European colonial rule in India once and for all.

Sino-Indian War (1962)

Though the Sino-Indian War of 1962 ended in a setback for India, the Indian Army's heroism during the conflict deserves recognition. Despite being caught off guard by China’s invasion, Indian soldiers displayed incredible courage while fighting in harsh, unfamiliar terrain at high altitudes. The Army's resilience and valor in the face of overwhelming odds highlighted the strength of India’s military, and in the aftermath, India reorganized its defense strategies, ensuring that future threats would be met with an even more determined and prepared force.

Indo-Pakistani War of 1965

In the Indo-Pakistani War of 1965, India bravely defended its territory when Pakistan launched Operation Gibraltar, aiming to infiltrate Jammu and Kashmir. Despite Pakistan's surprise attack, India quickly mobilized its forces, successfully countering the infiltration and fighting back with determination. The Indian Army demonstrated incredible resilience, repelling Pakistani forces across key battlefields, including Chamb and Akhnur. Though the war ended in a ceasefire, India's military prowess and strategic victory proved its ability to protect its sovereignty and defend against external aggression.

1967 Sino-Indian Conflict

The 1967 Sino-Indian conflict occurred along the Nathu La and Cho La passes in the Himalayan region of Sikkim, which was then a protectorate of India. Tensions between India and China had been simmering since the 1962 Sino-Indian War, and the situation escalated when China started asserting control over the disputed territories. In September 1967, clashes broke out between Indian and Chinese forces at Nathu La, a key mountain pass that linked Tibet with Sikkim. The skirmishes led to heavy casualties on both sides, with India successfully defending its position despite the Chinese forces' numerical advantage.

Following the Nathu La clash, hostilities continued at Cho La in October 1967, but the conflict did not escalate into a full-scale war. The conflict marked a significant turning point in Indo-China relations, as India demonstrated its military resilience and resolve. After the clashes, both sides agreed to a ceasefire, and diplomatic efforts were initiated to prevent further escalation. While the 1967 conflict was relatively contained, it reinforced India's determination to safeguard its territorial integrity and strengthened its defense posture in the region.

Operation against the Naxalites during 1971

In 1971, the Indian government launched an operation against the Naxalites, a group of Marxist-Leninist insurgents who had been active since the late 1960s. The Naxalite movement, named after the village of Naxalbari in West Bengal where it originated, sought to overthrow the Indian government through armed struggle, targeting rural areas and marginalized communities. The movement was particularly active in states like West Bengal, Bihar, Andhra Pradesh, and Odisha.

The government’s response to the Naxalite threat in 1971 was part of a broader effort to curb the growing insurgency. The operation aimed to suppress Naxalite activities by deploying police and military forces to dismantle their strongholds, disrupt their supply lines, and eliminate key leaders.

Bangladesh Liberation War of 1971

The Bangladesh Liberation War of 1971 was a pivotal conflict that led to the creation of Bangladesh as an independent nation. The war was triggered by the Bangladesh Liberation movement, seeking independence from Pakistan. The crisis escalated after the 1970 elections, when the Awami League, led by Sheikh Mujibur Rahman, won a majority, but the Pakistani military junta refused to transfer power.

In March 1971, Pakistan launched Operation Searchlight, a brutal crackdown on Bengali nationalists, which led to widespread atrocities. India, which had supported the Bengali independence movement, intervened in December 1971 after millions of refugees crossed into India, and Pakistan’s actions further destabilized the region.

Operation Pawan (1987–1990)

Operation Pawan (1987–1990) was an Indian military intervention in Sri Lanka during the Sri Lankan Civil War. India deployed the Indian Peace Keeping Force (IPKF) to enforce the Indo-Sri Lanka Accord, which aimed to bring peace between the Sri Lankan government and Tamil separatists. The operation began in 1987 when the Indian Army landed in Sri Lanka to disarm the Tamil Tigers (LTTE) and restore order. However, the IPKF soon faced fierce resistance from the LTTE, leading to heavy casualties. Despite initial efforts, the operation struggled to achieve its objectives, and tensions between the Indian forces and the Sri Lankan military grew. By 1990, after facing significant challenges, the IPKF was withdrawn, marking the end of the operation.

Kargil War - 1999

In 1998, India conducted nuclear tests, followed by Pakistan's own tests, giving both countries nuclear deterrence. India had tested a hydrogen bomb, which Pakistan lacked. Diplomatic tensions eased after the Lahore Summit in 1999, but were reignited when Pakistani paramilitary forces and Kashmiri insurgents captured strategic Himalayan heights in Kargil, vacated by the Indian Army due to winter. These heights overlooked the important Srinagar–Leh Highway (NH 1A), which was vital for India.

The Indian Army quickly mobilized 200,000 troops and launched Operation Vijay to reclaim the positions. However, the Pakistani forces had the advantage, controlling the heights and using artillery to target the NH 1A, making supply lines difficult for India. The priority for the Indian Army was to recapture key peaks, starting with Tiger Hill and Tololing in Dras. Point 4590, which had the best view of NH 1A, was recaptured by India on 14 June, marking a significant victory.
